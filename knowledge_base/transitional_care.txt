DOCUMENT: Transitional Care Management Services — CMS Medicare Learning Network Booklet (MLN908628, August 2025)

WHAT IT IS: Transitional Care Management (TCM) is a Medicare-covered service that supports patients moving back to a community setting after discharge from an inpatient or partial hospitalization stay. The TCM period spans 30 days, beginning on the discharge date and continuing through the next 29 days. Eligible discharge settings include inpatient acute care hospitals, inpatient psychiatric hospitals, inpatient rehabilitation facilities, long-term care hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, hospital outpatient observation or partial hospitalization programs, and partial hospitalization at community mental health centers. After discharge, the patient must return to a community setting such as a home, group home, boarding house, nursing facility, or assisted living facility. TCM requires that the treating professional accept care of the patient without a service gap and take responsibility for ongoing management. Patients must have moderate or high complexity medical decision making needs. The three core TCM components are interactive contact within 2 business days of discharge, non-face-to-face services throughout the 30-day period, and at least one face-to-face visit within the required timeframe. Medication reconciliation and management must also be completed on or before the face-to-face visit.

WHO QUALIFIES: TCM services may be provided by physicians of any specialty and by non-physician practitioners (NPPs) who are legally authorized to practice in their state, including certified nurse-midwives, clinical nurse specialists, nurse practitioners, and physician assistants. Auxiliary personnel working under general physician or NPP supervision may perform certain non-face-to-face tasks such as patient communication, medication management support, caregiver education, and community resource coordination. NPPs may also provide non-face-to-face TCM services incident to a physician's services. Only one physician or NPP may bill TCM services per patient per TCM period. The face-to-face visit requirement determines which CPT code applies: CPT 99495 requires at least moderate medical decision making and a face-to-face visit within 14 calendar days of discharge, while CPT 99496 requires high-level medical decision making and a face-to-face visit within 7 calendar days. Both codes can be delivered via telehealth.

BILLING AND OPERATIONS: TCM services are reported once per patient per 30-day period. The billing practitioner must document the patient discharge date, the date of first interactive contact with the patient or caregiver, the face-to-face visit date, and the level of medical decision making (moderate or high). The face-to-face visit cannot occur on the same day as hospital or observation discharge day management services, even when billed by the same provider. TCM cannot be billed if any part of the 30-day period falls within a post-operative global surgery period for a procedure billed by the same practitioner. Certain other care management codes may be billed concurrently with TCM when medically necessary and when time and effort are not double-counted, including ESRD services, chronic care management codes, remote physiologic and therapeutic monitoring codes, and hospice or home health supervision codes. If two or more contact attempts are made but unsuccessful, the provider may still bill TCM as long as the face-to-face visit occurs within the required timeframe and all other requirements are met.

RELEVANCE TO PAYMENT INTEGRITY: TCM carries several documented compliance risk areas. Improper billing can occur when the 2-business-day interactive contact requirement is not met, when the face-to-face visit falls outside the required window, when TCM is billed during a global surgery period, or when more than one practitioner bills TCM for the same patient in the same period. Documentation gaps are a key audit target: records must substantiate the discharge date, first contact date, face-to-face visit date, and the applicable level of medical decision making. Billing TCM without evidence of the required medication reconciliation is also a potential denial trigger. Concurrent billing of care management codes requires careful time-tracking to avoid double-counting, which is a known area of scrutiny under Medicare payment integrity reviews.

RELEVANCE TO CARE MANAGEMENT: TCM directly addresses the post-acute transition period, one of the highest-risk windows for preventable readmissions. The structure of TCM — early contact within 2 business days, non-face-to-face coordination across the 30-day period, and a required in-person or telehealth visit — mirrors best practices for reducing care gaps after hospitalization. The requirement for moderate or high medical decision making ensures TCM targets medically complex patients rather than routine discharges. The concurrent billing allowance for chronic care management codes signals that TCM is designed to integrate with longitudinal care programs for patients managing multiple chronic conditions. CMS also provides equity-focused resources tied to TCM, including guidance on reducing readmission disparities among racially and ethnically diverse Medicare populations, underscoring TCM's role as a health equity intervention as well as a reimbursement mechanism.
